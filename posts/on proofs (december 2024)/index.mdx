If it's not clear yet, I am quite fond of math. I mean, I've always liked it and have tried my best to get deep exposure into it. This has realized itself in a few different ways, particularly taking difficult classes and doing math competitions. I don't think I'm that good at math, but I still push myself to learn more because I truly do enjoy it. 

Sometimes when concepts related to math don't click super quickly to me, I go into a semi-existential-crisis mode and think about how I got to that point in my life. That's sort of unclear, but what I mean is that I think about how I've learned math until that point and whether or not I've been making correct decisions that have helped me grow as a learner. Oftentimes, I will pinpoint the fact that I usually did not accepts proofs as a way to learn math. In high school, I was at a point where I only cared about getting correct answers to numerical questions (such as on AMC, AIME, and most of my math classes). Looking at back at some of my proofs from 10th grade, I realize that they were utterly **horrible** and I really did not understand math at all.

I think this came from an idea of proofs being a separate entity from general mathematics.
